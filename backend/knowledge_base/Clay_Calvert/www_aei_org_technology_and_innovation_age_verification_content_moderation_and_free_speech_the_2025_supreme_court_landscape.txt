	Age Verification, Content Moderation, and Free Speech: The 2025 Supreme Court Landscape | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

On July 9, 2025, AEI hosted an expert panel that examined the US Supreme Court’s recent First Amendment decisions affecting free speech and technology policy, including the 2025 rulings in
Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton
and
TikTok v. Garland.
The panel featured TechFreedom’s
Corbin Barthold
, AEI’s
Daniel Lyons
, and the Center for Democracy & Technology’s
Kate Ruane
, with AEI’s
Clay Calvert
moderating.
Below is an edited and abridged transcript of key highlights from the discussion. You can

Clay Calvert:
In
Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton
, the six-justice conservative majority applied the deferential standard of judicial review called intermediate scrutiny to examine and uphold a Texas online age-verification statute. The majority held that the government can force adults to disclose personal, age-verifying information in order to access sexual content they have a First Amendment right to view but that is forbidden for minors. Justice Kagan penned a dissent, joined by Justices Sotomayor and Jackson, explaining that they would have applied the rigorous, strict scrutiny standard of review because it’s clearly a content-based statute, as it only applies to a specific type of subject matter, sexual speech. The dissent would have sent the case back to the lower courts to apply strict scrutiny. Kate, what is your reaction to the court’s ruling?
Kate Ruane:
In my view, the majority opinion is, as Justice Kagan says in her dissent, at war with itself. It very much wants to reach the result of upholding this law. And at one point in the opinion, it specifically says, well, we can’t apply strict scrutiny, even though we’ve applied strict scrutiny to very similar—indeed, at one point, nearly the exact same attempt at restricting speech—cases. We can’t do it now because we can make a few distinctions that we think justify applying intermediate scrutiny here. The way it reads is: We can’t apply strict scrutiny because we know the law would fail strict scrutiny, so we’re going to apply intermediate scrutiny and not rational basis because we also acknowledge that there is some speech interest that is being restricted here, and that adults are now burdened in accessing content that is constitutionally protected as to them. I think that this decision in general—finding that a law that has previously been subject to strict scrutiny and is clearly content based, would not survive strict scrutiny, but somehow we have come up with a way to say that, the speech at issue is partially protected (in that it is not protected as to minors, but is protected as to adults) now gets intermediate scrutiny—could open the door to increased use of intermediate scrutiny for content-based restrictions on speech. And I’m really concerned about that.
Justice Kagan certainly thinks that the majority knew the result that it wanted to reach and says so in her dissenting opinion. I can’t speak for them. I am not in their brains, but when reading the discussion of why they can’t apply strict scrutiny to this particular statute, that is part of what I see—that this was a results-driven decision, at least to some degree, because they do want to allow the application of age-verification requirements for access to this content. I think Justice Kagan argues that they could have done that. They could have given guidance for how to do that in a manner that survives strict scrutiny. But that’s not what happened. There was a desire to uphold this particular statute.
Daniel Lyons
: On the tiers of scrutiny point, I think Justice Kagan’s dissent definitely is the better of the argument. The majority is trying to present the Texas law as just a restriction on minors who don’t have a First Amendment right to access this material that’s obscene as to minors. But the law isn’t requiring kids to produce ID before they can go on the sites, it’s requiring adults to produce ID before they can go on these sites. Maybe that’s okay, maybe it’s not, but it’s definitely a condition on—and therefore a direct regulation of—adult speech on the basis of the content that adults are trying to access. So, under very basic First Amendment rules, this should have been a strict scrutiny case.
Clay Calvert:
What about age verification itself? What are the issues with these systems?
Kate Ruane:
The court doesn’t discuss the process of age verification at all. They kind of say age verification is a thing now, and it’s fine. But the thing is, in the Center for Democracy & Technology’s brief, we looked at all of the various age-verification technologies that we thought might be permissible to use to satisfy the Texas statute. We found problems with every single one of them, either because they could be evaded by minors who are able to get somebody else’s ID and use it to verify themselves, or because they have an inaccuracy rate that will deny adults, particularly adults in marginalized populations. Particularly adults with disabilities, adults who are LGBTQ, particularly transgender and nonbinary people, and adults of color are more likely to experience error rates in a lot of these age-verification methods. You don’t just show your ID and it’s fine, the way that it might be in a brick-and-mortar store, where the data also disappears because there’s no permanence in that transaction.
And what happens if you don’t have an ID? Lots of people do not have access to government-issued identification that proves their age. That’s another problem. If you use one of these methods that guess your age, every single age assurance method, like facial recognition or the use of transaction data, which is what the Texas law talks about, is a guess. It is a guess at how old you are based on your face or your activity. Oftentimes, if you are guessed to fall in a certain age range—so let’s say 17 to 25—the idea then is you get shunted into another process where you verify your age by showing your government-issued ID. And what if you don’t have one?
Clay Calvert
: Instead of the government imposing age-verification mandates, what about the alternative of parents using things like filtering software to protect minors? How do we balance adults’ rights to receive speech with protecting minors?
Daniel Lyons
: I’m not as optimistic about parents’ ability to protect children from stuff they don’t want their children to see online. That having been said, you’re never going to get a perfect calibration. You can either set a legal limit in a way that maximizes adult consumption of content and risks some children seeing stuff they’re not supposed to see. Or you can maximize the protection of children at a cost of some adults not being able to see what they want to see. Historically, we’ve set the line on a speech maximization––we’ll make sure that what we’re doing is protecting adult rights to view content, even if it means some kids wind up getting around the restriction that we don’t want them to have. And that’s kind of what the least restrictive means analysis requires. That’s why strict scrutiny would have been a death knell for this passing the court if it adopted that. This is the first decision I think that goes the other way.
Corbin Barthold
: I just can’t get over the tailoring problem with the law. This law doesn’t apply to social media and it doesn’t apply to search engines. The majority decision says that it was reasonable for Texas to think that the one-third threshold (of adult content on a website) is what it takes to protect children. The question is, is there enough explicit content on social media or a search engine to satiate a minor’s desire to see it? I’m sure the answer is yes. So, it goes back to the point of just totally gutting the intermediate scrutiny test.
Clay Calvert:
In January, the Court ruled in
TikTok v. Garland
. Congress passed the Protecting Americans from Foreign Adversary Controlled Applications Act on a bipartisan basis during the Biden administration. Under the Act, either TikTok must divest itself of Chinese ownership or else the American companies and app stores that service TikTok and provide different support for it can no longer do business with TikTok. The Court, as it did in
Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton
, applied intermediate scrutiny to uphold the law. President Trump has now delayed its application and enforcement a couple of times. What does the ruling say about free speech?
Corbin Barthold:
Young people of America, who make up a lot of this audience: This law is aimed at you. It’s not aimed at me. It’s not aimed at Daniel, or Kate, or Clay, or Shane. It’s not aimed at boomers. The legislature was afraid that you will be exposed to ideas that are dangerous and that will get into your head and infect you like a hypodermic needle, and then you will believe bad things. That is what drove this law. Now, there are all kinds of problems with whether it’s actually true that the algorithm is manipulated. We are in a republic where we are considered citizens who can make up our own minds based on the ideas we are exposed to. This law was about legislators thinking that it is no longer true for you. That was the key to this case. The case is not about TikTok. The case is about you.
Daniel Lyons:
I was on record as saying the biggest mistake in the evolution of the litigation of this case was combining TikTok’s own suit with the suit of TikTok’s users because the TikTok users, I thought, had a much stronger case, and they just got lost in the shuffle. I have lots to say, but I’ll limit myself to a couple of things.
One, the court decided this on content-neutral grounds because it says we think Congress would have passed this just on the data privacy rationale––the idea that TikTok hoovers up your data that can have intelligence value to the Chinese government. That’s a sufficient rationale for the court, so it decided the case on intermediate scrutiny grounds. I’m not sure that’s correct. Representative Gallagher has gone on record as saying the TikTok bill actually stalled in Congress for a while and that what got it the votes to get over the line was the proliferation of pro-Palestinian videos that were popping up after October 7. The videos enraged enough of the holdouts in Congress to provide the votes that got the bill over the line in the House. That tells me that it was a content-based decision that allowed the bill to get a majority vote in the House.
Second, I think it’s really problematic that the government has all this national security information, and they rushed the case through the court. Justice Gorsuch’s concurrence makes very clear he’s uncomfortable with how they rushed it, but it’s important enough to make this get done. And then we get the decision: In my sense, it’s like the worst of all possible worlds. I’m not a TikTok fan. I see the case for national security. I totally understand that, if TikTok knows information about this person or that person, this has intelligence value that they could use. I get that. I wasn’t pro-TikTok, I was pro-First Amendment. And so now it’s the worst possible outcome where we’ve got this decision that harmed First Amendment values without meeting the national security concerns because TikTok is still out there and available.
Clay Calvert:
Let’s move to
Murthy v. Missouri
, which involved the Biden administration’s jawboning of social media platforms to take down conservative, right-leaning, right-tilting content about things like mask mandates, COVID-19 policies, school shutdowns, and election fraud. And the Supreme Court last year punted on the merits of that case—the majority said that the plaintiffs didn’t have standing to get to the substantive merit. Might we see another future jawboning case involving other types of informal efforts by the government to try to change or censor speech?
Corbin Barthold:
I would quibble with the notion that the court punted. The plaintiffs lost because they weren’t the victims of what they claimed to be the victim of. The Biden administration was jawboning social media platforms about speech primarily involving COVID. I’m not glad that they did that. I don’t support them doing that. But the tech platforms are big boys, and largely did their own thing and made their independent decisions about their content moderation. And so the people who were complaining, “Oh, I was silenced by the government,” there just wasn’t evidence for that. They weren’t able to make the causal connection.
Now, some of the same actors who were really jazzed about this jawboning problem in the prior administration are now gleeful jawboners themselves. The Federal Communications Commission and the Federal Trade Commission are going after parties that have crossed President Donald Trump in some way, or have engaged in their own speech rights toward social media in some way. But all we see is caving and capitulation all the way up to the horizon.
Daniel Lyons
: Like a lot of things, Trump is taking what’s come before him and turning it to 11.
Kate Ruane:
Jawboning isn’t new. But in this particular circumstance, the government is making it very clear what speech it doesn’t want to hear. It might be speech that is critical of President Trump; it might be a speech that is critical of Israel; it might be other types of speech that the government is now categorizing as anti-American. And the administration is using every lever possible in order to create examples of people who have engaged in speech that the government doesn’t like, and are punishing those people sort of in the open so that everyone else understands that they shouldn’t say those things. So, if you want to be a student at Harvard, and you are a foreign national, you are currently probably scrubbing your social media accounts or deleting them entirely.
With a lot of the examples that we’ve talked about so far, we’re trying to achieve what some people might disagree with, but is a legitimate end. This, to me, is very different, especially in how clear the administration is being in so many different ways about the type of speech it does not want to see produced anymore.
