	The Crucial Role of Mid-Band Spectrum for 5G and 6G Networks | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

The United States is facing a challenge in spectrum management. The country once led the world in digital innovation, but now it is playing catch-up to China. China has been more proactive in allocating mid-band spectrum, which is essential for wireless communication and mobile technology. The US needs to take steps to ensure that it has enough spectrum to meet the needs of both its citizens and businesses.
According to the
GSMA
, an organization representing mobile network operators worldwide, the mid-band spectrum holds significant economic value compared to the low and high bands in the context of 5G and 6G networks. They
predict
that by 2030, the mid-band spectrum will contribute to a global GDP increase of over $610 billion, accounting for approximately 65 percent of the total socio-economic benefits generated by 5G. However, there is a
concern
that without adequate availability of mid-band spectrum, we may lose 40 percent of this impact, amounting to a staggering $360 billion loss by 2030.
Via Twenty20
The Government Accountability Office (GAO)
notes
that mid-band spectrum’s congestion leads to delays in the planning and deployment of 5G and has recommended more mid-band spectrum be accessible for commercial use. Back in 2020 the GAO
recommendations
stated:
The Chairman of FCC should develop, in coordination with NTIA and other relevant stakeholders, specific and measurable performance goals—with related strategies and measures—to manage spectrum demands associated with 5G deployment.
Yet, as of March 2023, the GAO notes this inter-agency planning process was still
looking
to set “achievable goals” to address these challenges. It is imperative that the US agencies involved in the deployment of 5G agree to an explicit plan for a better balance in spectrum allocations.
The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) and the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA) must prioritize the allocation of
full power, licensed spectrum
to match the unlicensed and Wi-Fi spectrum.
Experts
suggest that over 1500 megahertz of full power, licensed spectrum is required to preserve the economic and technological advantages of a fully deployed 5G infrastructure.
Recognizing the significance of mid-band spectrum, China has
taken
proactive measures. Beginning July 1, 2023, Chinese network operators have designated spectrum in the 6 gigahertz bands specifically for mid-band 5G and 6G networks. China has been
coordinating
their mid-band spectrum for over a decade and constructing cell sites at a rapid pace to utilize over 300 megahertz of mid-band spectrum for 5G. In contrast, the United States faces potential risks to its national and economic security due to the allocation priorities of its regulatory agencies.
The Brattle Group’s
report
finds that “the US needs 400 megahertz of full power, licensed spectrum in the next five years to meet projected demand.” This disparity in the allocation of spectrum between licensed and unlicensed usage in the US is creating a lag in the planning for the timely availability of spectrum needed to power mobile services. The US government has
assigned
seven times more spectrum for Wi-Fi and unlicensed applications compared to supporting commercial networks. Additionally, if the US lags behind China in deploying advanced wireless networks and devices, there will be substantial economic ramifications.
Failing to rectify the current spectrum imbalance could have far-reaching implications for national security. As Clete Johnson from the Center for Strategic and International Studies
notes
in the first of a series of papers focusing on 5G and security, this disparity raises long-term concerns regarding capacity for data gathering and other national security and economic implications. The situation also warrants an examination of the
consequences
associated with China’s adoption of the 6 gigahertz spectrum for their mid-band needs.
US agencies should therefore prioritize the allocation of full power, licensed spectrum for 5G and 6G networks while also instating innovative approaches facilitating coexistence between incumbents and new market entrants within occupied spectrum bands. By fostering collaboration and finding common ground, we can ensure the successful development and deployment of future wireless technologies.
We can safeguard our technological leadership and national security with more efficient use of our spectrum assets. More mobile spectrum is a key catalyst for socio-economic progress and development and more high-speed networks are indispensable for fostering a thriving and resilient economy. In this high-stakes game of invisible real estate, delay could spell disaster for our national and economic security, and success could secure our leadership for the future of a well-structured wireless world.
