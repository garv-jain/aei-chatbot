	How Software ‘Nutrition Labels’ Can Enhance Cybersecurity: Highlights from My Conversation with Allan Friedman | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

By Shane Tews
Following a wave of recent attacks, cyber and supply chain
security are high on the United States’ policy agenda. Software “nutrition
labels” are one solution cyber experts are considering; what do these “labels”
look like, and how can government and industry work together to ensure their
effectiveness?
Allan Friedman
, director of cybersecurity initiatives at the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA), joined “Explain to Shane” to discuss how
Software Bills of Materials
(SBOMs) can enrich America’s cyber toolbox, and how NTIA can promote their use without direct mandates. Allan and I also discussed the shared responsibilities industry and government have when it comes to cyber.

Shane Tews
:
Allan, start us off by explaining what an
SBOM is.
Allan Friedman: An SBOM is a Software Bill of Materials. Essentially,
you can think of it as a list of ingredients for software. One of the core
questions around security and technology today is: Why don’t we have that level
of transparency for the software that runs our lives and our critical
infrastructure? The vision behind a Software Bill of Materials is just to have
that level of transparency — making sure we have some data about what’s in the
software we use.
If we wanted to torture our ingredients list metaphor, some
of those ingredients are fresh and healthy and nutritious, but some of them are
stale and rotten and may have things that are bad for us. So as we try to
understand what healthy or “hygienic” software looks like, the first step is
transparency. The first step in an efficient marketplace for quality and secure
software is to know what we have.
Now, having this data won’t solve all of our problems, just
as looking at a list of ingredients won’t magically allow me to stick with some
diet I’ve committed to. It does, however, give me the tools to think about what’s
there and make an informed decision. You and I might be okay with ingesting
something containing beef tallow, but people with certain restrictions or dietary
requirements may not be. So this is about giving people the tools and data to
make those decisions.
How are you working
to ensure folks in industry will not only adopt SBOMs, but also cooperate with
one another?
We call this the multistakeholder process. The model is such
that the government doesn’t have a vested interest in what the solution is; we
just need there to be a solution. And so our approach is to bring as many
perspectives to the table as possible and help the community find a solution.
For this case, actually defining what we wanted to fix was
the first step. We kind of knew most people would understand the SBOM idea and
would have a broad idea of whether they were for or against it. So the first
thing we had to do was put everything on the table and make sure we had a
shared vision for what a minimum viable SBOM was.
Building on that, we then had to take inventory of what we
had to work with and identify risks and challenges. A good analogy for one of
these challenges is the term “. . . and natural flavorings” on an ingredients
label. Even if I have the full list of ingredients, it doesn’t tell me the recipe.
There’s a big difference between knowing what’s in something and being able to
replicate it. (I can’t make an Oreo just by looking at the list of ingredients.)
So for an SBOM, we had to decide whether that type of “. . . and natural
flavorings” ambiguity was something we could work with.
This would basically allow people to say, “There’s still
some stuff I’m not going to tell you, but I have to tell you what I’m not going
to tell you.” And that’s something we built into our model: the idea of “known
unknowns.” Whenever you tell someone what’s in your software, you also have to
tell them what you’re
not
revealing.
You have to tell them some information about what you’re not telling, which
could be because you don’t know, right? You’re using some other supplier’s software,
because it’s transitive. It’s a big tree.
Or it could be that you simply aren’t comfortable sharing
that data, but in either case, now your customer or your downstream user can
say, “Now that I know about this lack of understanding, we can have a
negotiation” or “tell me more” or “this is fine” based on their risk. I can
hire a third-party security tester to make sure there’s nothing extra risky
there. And that’s okay. Again, the starting point is this notion of
transparency.
So how exactly do you
quantify the “known unknowns”? And how do SBOM users decide what “ingredients”
they are and aren’t going to define?
One way to think about our software is as a tree. At the
base of the tree, we have our software, and then we branch up. The first branch
is the operating system; then, the operating system in turn uses a
cryptographic library, and the cryptographic library in turn uses several
mathematical functions.
Ideally, SBOMs would give people that full tree. But the “known
unknown” idea addresses the possibility of reaching a point in that tree where
you simply can’t go any further because there are secrets or you simply don’t
have the necessary information. In that case, you’d just apply a label that
says “this is unknown. I don’t know if this component has any further
dependencies or not.” And that way, someone knows what they’re agreeing
to.
But if you’re using an operating system that’s commercial or
proprietary and you haven’t been able to get the SBOM for that, you can just
tell your customer you’re using this operating system, and now they can decide
whether or not it’s something they’re worried about.
What different
industries are coming to the table for this?
We’re seeing interest across the board, especially as more communities
realize their software supply chain risks. This is an area in which we’re
trying to help industry help itself. But as you know, NITA doesn’t have
regulatory authority. Our vision is to try to help the community come together.
But it’s also a space in which having regulators imply this is coming will help
industry move quicker.
The Food and Drug Administration announced in 2018 that they
were requiring new medical devices to have SBOMs, but basically said “we’re not
going to define this. We want the medical device manufacturers to work with the
rest of the software world in the NTIA process.”
And this has remained a key priority for not just the
medical device manufacturers; many of these companies were actively involved in
the production race for vaccines, personal protective equipment, ventilators,
etc.
But it has moved on beyond that. We’ve got some folks in the
automotive sector thinking about how this looks between those who make car parts
and those who assemble the car. In both of these cases, we like how the proof
of concept model allows people to experiment and try this with live data.
They’re talking about real products and live use cases.
We’ve also just launched a third pilot effort in partnership
with the Department of Energy and the National Labs to think about this for the
energy and bulk power sectors.
You’ve explained how
NTIA is encouraging the adoption of SBOMs, but doesn’t have the actual
regulatory power to mandate them. So what are your next steps to continue this
process?
Our vision is to continue being the nexus of these
discussions. Ultimately, different sectors are going to have to take this and
make it their own. There’s a real difference between the needs of the
healthcare sector, the automotive sector, and the financial sector. They each
have their own requirements. We just want to make sure people feel comfortable
adapting so everyone follows the same basic model — so that there’s
interoperability.
We have some existing technical standards today that we want
to encourage people to use rather than going off and bending something new. Some
people may want more data, but we’ve emphasized the importance of a minimum
viable model. One of our mantras is “crawl, then walk, then run.”
Our vision is to have a point at which everyone who’s
interested in this can learn about it and build on it. That’s the idea of
having an SBOM nexus where the entire community knows this is where the status
quo of SBOM is.
Our final approach at NTIA is to work with international
partners. As you know, NTIA is built around some assumptions that the internet
is global and doesn’t belong to any one country. We’re partnering with folks in
Japan who are very vested in thinking about this for supply chain management. And
I recently chatted with one of our counterparts in England who works with their
national security and cybersecurity center.
Similarly, in Brussels and London and other capitals around the world, people are paying attention to supply chain. We want to make sure they’re aware SBOM exists, that there’s an active community, and that there are existing standards and governance. We want to make sure they know what resources are there, what’s been led by the industry, and how the community can build on what we already have.
