Event Summary
On December 14, AEI’s Brent Orrell hosted an event exploring whether there is a fundamental misunderstanding of what’s required to deter and prevent criminal behavior.
The American criminal justice system has tended to favor either a punishment or rehabilitation paradigm. Police, courts, and prisons exist to remove criminals from the streets, isolate them from the rest of society, and deter future criminal behavior. Prisons try to do this through the weight of incarceration or by intervening through various programs and mechanisms of social control to change behavior.
The panel examined a third approach, which leverages the internal psychological, emotional, and developmental processes common to all humans to help people transition from criminal to pro-social identities.
Shawn D. Bushway of the RAND Corporation laid out the case in his recent AEI report, “Reentry, desistance, and identity achievement,” arguing that supporting the development of pro-social identities — as a spouse, mother or father, worker, or student — can supplant and replace criminal identities and stop criminal behavior. The Brookings Institution’s Rashawn Ray, Bret Bucklen of the Pennsylvania Department of Corrections, and Julio Medina of Exodus Transitional Community responded to and expanded on Dr. Bushway’s ideas on desistance.
— Hunter Dixon
Event Description
Desistance, the decision to abandon criminal behavior, is one of the great puzzles in criminal justice policy. Shawn D. Bushway of the RAND Corporation argues that desistance depends on achieving a new, noncriminal, pro-social identity, which is often a long and arduous process supported by empirical evidence.
Join AEI’s Brent Orrell for a discussion on Dr. Bushway’s report “Reentry, desistance, and identity achievement.” The event features remarks from Dr. Bushway, followed by comments from senior criminal justice researchers and reentry practitioners, including Rashawn Ray of the Brookings Institution, Bret Bucklen of the Pennsylvania Department of Corrections, and Julio Medina of Exodus Transitional Community.
