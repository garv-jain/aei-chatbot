	Unemployment Insurance in the United Kingdom | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

On Friday,
The New York Times
ran a news
article
covering how sudden, mass layoffs were creating bottlenecks in state unemployment insurance programs. These systems, which had been in the process of whittling down their staffs in a three percent unemployment economy, are suddenly inundated with claims, with the wave likely to grow significantly higher in the coming weeks. Workers described sitting on web pages for hours and sometimes days in a futile effort to register for benefits. Another example of a rickety system designed in the days of the Great Depression being unable to fulfill its mandate at the very moment of greatest need.
Boris Johnson’s government has opted for a
different approach
. The UK government announced yesterday that it would support employers who wanted to “furlough” workers, paying up to 80 percent of salary for workers. This has two advantages to relying solely on traditional unemployment benefits. First, it maintains the connection between the firm and the worker with the expectation that once the crisis passes, these workers can return quickly to their jobs. Second, it leverages the existing employer wage and salary payment infrastructure to supplement the government unemployment benefit offices and registration and payment systems. Two birds, one stone.
As Congress considers emergency measures to deal with layoffs and unemployment, the Johnson approach needs to be carefully considered as a simple and effective approach to stabilizing the workforce for both its current income and future employment challenges.
