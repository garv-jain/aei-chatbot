	Skills for the 21st Century | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

Brookings scholar Harry Holzer just released an
interesting short piece
analyzing the potential impact of artificial intelligence on job displacement, in which he makes five recommendations for mitigating AI’s impact on middle-class job opportunities.
Educators at all levels should teach noncognitive skills, while continuing to foster narrower, in-demand technical skills that are connected to immediate employment opportunities. Image via Twenty20.
Summarizing OECD data on the topic, Holzer estimates that between 10 and 30 percent of US jobs are susceptible to partial or complete automation, with lower-skilled occupations at a much higher risk of complete displacement by AI. Higher skilled jobs — including attorneys and physicians — will likely face a squeeze as well as AI replaces some tasks within those jobs currently performed by humans with computers.
Holzer then makes five recommendations for preparing workers for the AI future, leading with the need to focus on “21st century skills.” These skills, Holzer says, “include communication and a range of social and interactive skills and various kinds of problem-solving abilities.” Such skills, he notes, are far less likely to be automated and having them makes it easier for workers to retrain for new jobs. He concludes this recommendation by pointing out that educators at all levels should focus on teaching these
noncognitive skills
, while, at the same time, continuing to foster narrower, in-demand technical skills that are connected to immediate employment opportunities.
There are no simple either-or propositions in the labor market future. Our policies and programs should reflect that reality.
