	‘What Is a Class Act?’ for $1000 | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

When we think
about the reentry of the formerly incarcerated into society, we spend time
thinking about housing, living, and employment, but we don’t spend too much
time considering the burdens to formerly incarcerated people getting those
kinds of things.
This is where Alex Trebek, the former host of Jeopardy!, comes in. Trebek passed away late last year. While much of the viewership was wondering who would replace him, his son and Jeopardy!’s costumer were wondering about something else — namely, what to do with all of Trebek’s suits, button-downs, ties, and other formalwear that the host had used and accumulated from 1984 (when he started as Jeopardy host) until 2020.
Flowers are placed on the star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame of TV host Alex Trebek in Los Angeles, California, U.S., November 8, 2020.  REUTERS/David Swanson
Trebek’s family decided to donate
the clothing
(14 suits, 58 dress shirts, 300 neckties, 25 polo shirts, 14 sweaters, nine sport coats, nine pairs of dress shoes, 15 belts, two parkas, three pairs of dress slacks, and more) to the Doe Fund’s
Ready, Willing, & Able
— an organization that helps those afflicted with substance abuse, as well as homeless and formerly incarcerated people, find work. It is a 12-month residential program that takes participants through goal setting, education, career identification and preparation, and job search leading to full-time employment and a transition to self-sufficiency.
Trebek’s clothing
went to Doe Fund participants for use in mock and real interviews, and presumably,
to start a work wardrobe after they landed jobs. It was an amazingly thoughtful
use of the resource and a lasting tribute to the man who wore the clothes
first.
In a
taped episode
that went live in January, Alex Trebek echoed George H. W. Bush about the need for a gentler, kinder society. May we all do the same.
