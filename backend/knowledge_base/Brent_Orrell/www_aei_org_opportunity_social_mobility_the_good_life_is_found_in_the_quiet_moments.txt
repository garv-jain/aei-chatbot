	The Good Life Is Found in the Quiet Moments | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

As we grapple with the costs and benefits of social
distancing, we may find a few quiet moments for reflecting on deeper questions that
are often pushed off in the rush to meet the moment-to-moment demands of
professional and personal life.
If you have some downtime while you walk on your treadmill, fold laundry, or cook dinner, I’d like to recommend my recently released
podcast interview
with Ryan Hanley on “
Hardly Working
”, a professor of political science at Boston College, discussing his recent book,
Our Great Purpose: Adam Smith on Living a Better Life
. For some, it will be counter-intuitive that the father of capitalism has much to say on living the “good life” beyond making money. Prior to writing
The Wealth of Nations
, Smith wrote a lesser-known volume,
The Theory of Moral Sentiments
, a philosophical inquiry into the sources of prosperity and human happiness. To an important degree, Smith believed the pursuit of wealth is a psychological bait-and-switch: The desire for it helps to improve economic well-being for others, but frequently leaves the ones pursuing it unhappy and dissatisfied. The real source of what he called “tranquility of mind” was deeper and often at odds with the getting and spending of money.
COVID-19 presents many challenges — and a few opportunities. Smith’s insights about the importance of family, friends, and community in achieving the good life are especially relevant right now. If we must “gear down” to a slower pace, it’s also true that we have a chance to invest in building the human relationships that are the true source of a fulfilling life.
