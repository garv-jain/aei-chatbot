	The ‘soft Racism’ of the COVID-19 Pandemic Response | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

On March 12, I published a piece in
The Hill
examining some of the challenges that low-income and minority communities were likely to face during the COVID-19 pandemic and the need to prepare the health and human services infrastructure serving those communities. Today, The Washington Post and The New York Times, as well as numerous other outlets, are reporting that African-Americans are being disproportionately infected and are suffering far higher mortality than white communities. These outcomes may not have been completely avoidable, but they were entirely foreseeable. The failure to prepare an in-depth response plan appears to be a form of the “soft racism” of invisibility and public indifference.
Let’s take a look at what
the data
are showing us about the impact of COVID-19 on African-Americans.  In Illinois, African-Americans make up 28 percent of all positive COVID tests and 43 percent of all COVID-19 deaths despite comprising just 15 percent of the population. In Michigan, where African-Americans make up just 14 percent of the population, they account for 40 percent of deaths. In Louisiana, 70 percent of COVID deaths are among African-Americans, who make up 32 percent of the state’s population.
People line up early in the morning to be tested for coronavirus disease (COVID-19) outside Elmhurst Hospital Center in the Queens borough of New York City, U.S., March 26, 2020.  REUTERS/Stefan Jeremiah
The state of Wisconsin merits some special attention this week for its mishandling of social distancing in the context of the racial disparities of COVID-19. As the state went ahead yesterday with its presidential primary and hotly contested state Supreme Court election, it opened just five polling places for the entire city of Milwaukee, thus guaranteeing that voters faced a choice between exercising their franchise and exposing themselves to disease. For African-American voters, this was an especially burdensome choice.
Milwaukee County
is just 28 percent African-American, yet African-Americans make up almost half of all COVID-19 infections and over 70 percent of the recorded COVID-19 deaths. Meanwhile the
Speaker of the Wisconsin House of Representatives
appeared on television, in full personal protective gear, insisting it was safe to vote and encouraging voters to turn out. This is not the leadership we need as we fight a life-threatening pandemic illness.
As I noted in The Hill, the disproportionate impact of COVID-19 on African-Americans was not entirely avoidable. Comorbidities play a huge role in creating vulnerability to serious and fatal cases. A host of underlying health conditions endemic in the African-American community — obesity, diabetes, high blood pressure, kidney disease, and heart disease — increase the likelihood of complications and death. The virus doesn’t create these health conditions; it exploits them ruthlessly. However, there are other factors that exacerbate risk that we do have control over: access to health care, disparities in diagnosis and treatment, and poor public health communication to name a few. Months of delay in preparing for the pandemic has left those who were always going to be most vulnerable at even higher levels of risk.
In the midst of this crisis, our options for improving assistance to African-American and other disadvantaged populations are constrained. As I outlined in my earlier piece, it is both possible and essential to provide poor communities with public health information and, where possible, testing and other health care resources via faith-based and other community institutions as well as public agencies. Increasing funding for federally funded community health centers is also essential, especially since
many such centers have had to lay-off
workers as they have shifted to providing mostly COVID-19-related services.
Beyond these immediate steps, any “after-action” study of the COVID-19 crisis needs a sharp focus on the relationship between race, illness, and the responses of federal, state, and local governments in preparing for and responding to the pandemic. Like the response to Hurricane Katrina, we have thus far come up short, and we are running out of excuses.
