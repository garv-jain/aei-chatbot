	Down on the Farm - with Robots | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

Anyone who has ever labored under the summer sun in an agricultural job (raises hand) can appreciate the import of yesterday’s
Washington Post’s report
on the rise of agricultural robots. A decline in immigration and an unwillingness of Americans to take jobs picking fruit and vegetables has put enormous pressure on farmers who risk seeing their crops rot in the field unless an alternative to human labor is found.
Via Agrobot
The Post story is a case study in the promise of artificial intelligence and automation: a pressing need (labor shortages) drives innovation which advances in fits and starts slowly replacing human labor with machines. In strawberry fields and apple orchards, engineers are experimenting with technology that will substitute machines for human muscle in the pain-staking task of picking ripe produce without damaging it in the process. Right now, a strawberry picking machine, using photography and sensors, can identify and collect just 20 percent of the fruit while human hands can gather 80 percent — but that differential is sure to shrink over time as technology improves. One apple farmer is quoted as saying that he wanted the robots “yesterday” to eliminate the headache of finding and keeping pickers.
One question that immediately arises is what happens to those who are currently making $12 to $15 per hour in the fields and orchards? The extant labor shortage suggests there are fewer and fewer people to displace. Consistent with the history of this type of innovation, the article also says many of the workers who currently do the picking will eventually be employed cleaning and maintaining the equipment, jobs that require higher skill (and wage) levels while reducing the wear and tear on knees, backs and shoulders. These benefits are so significant that even the United Farm Workers of America are in favor of expanded use of agricultural robots.
The Post’s report is a healthy reminder that, speaking from experience, some of the tasks robots will take are eminently worth giving up: routine, exhausting, and often debilitating work demanding minimal skill and providing subsistence wages. It is also a prime example of how the advance of technology spins off new and better jobs in the process. Yes, the robots are coming, but they are nice, friendly, and helpful and may make us richer to boot.
