	Disadvantaged Americans Need Attention amid This Health Crisis | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

The New York Times ran an
important piece
earlier this week about the challenges low income communities face in the event of a widespread coronavirus outbreak. Federal, state, and local policymakers and program leaders should pay close attention to it.
Most negative social trends find themselves amplified in poor communities. The country catches a cold and the poor come down with pneumonia, which might be uncomfortably precise in the case of coronavirus. Underlying health conditions of the type that turn coronavirus from several days of misery into a life threatening illness are more present among the poor. Asthma, heart disease, kidney disease, lung problems related to smoking, and other chronic conditions are all far more prevalent in these individuals. Health services are also
less accessible
, for reasons of cost or location, leading low income families to postpone necessary treatment.
As the New York Times points out, an even more troubling aspect of this challenge is the high level of mistrust some minority communities, especially African Americans, have for the
health system
itself. A troubling history of racism and exploitation has sometimes led minorities to avoid doctors and distrust medical treatment. Infamies like the Tuskegee syphilis study have helped to embed suspicion among African Americans that they might be used as “guinea pigs” for new treatments and vaccines.
