	A Cheerful Heart Is Good Medicine | American Enterprise Institute - AEI

In 2011, John Kralik released a powerful memoir entitled
A Simple Act of Gratitude: How Learning to Say Thank You Changed My Life
. In it, Kralik describes a tragic period in his life when everything was falling apart, including his work, relationships, and physical health. With little else to turn to, he determined to write one thank you note every day for a year to a different person who had impacted him in some way. To his surprise, this simple decision transformed his life.
Research shows thankfulness carries more punch than the “good vibes” it is often associated with. Among the
benefits
of
gratitude
:
Improved physical health and mental well-being
Higher levels of happiness and life satisfaction
Less focus on material well-being
Lower levels of work burnout
Better sleep and less cellular inflammation
Less depression, less fatigue, and more resilience
to trauma
One does not need to
feel
grateful to begin perpetuating a grateful spirit.
Studies have shown
that simple steps of disciplined gratitude — such as keeping a daily gratitude journal — can lead to considerable psychological improvements, providing a small way forward for many of us who are experiencing the stresses of social isolation.
This is an ancient truth. Nearly 3,000 years ago, King Solomon put it this way: “A cheerful heart is good medicine, but a crushed spirit dries up the bones.” This is the kind of medicine we can all use right now.
